Strategic Context

Two business models. One customer base. Competing objectives.

As HP's subscription ink service expanded into developed markets, the subscription business team and the traditional supplies portfolio team were both targeting the same printer owners — but with fundamentally different incentives about which customers to pursue and how aggressively.

Subscription Team Objective
Maximize subscriber count & penetration rate

From the subscription team's perspective, high-usage, high-loyalty customers were the ideal target. The cost-savings value proposition was strongest for heavy users, and loyal customers were less likely to churn. Leadership also preferred subscription economics — 100% OEM loyalty, predictable monthly revenue, reduced balance sheet seasonality.

Portfolio Strategy Perspective
Protect the most valuable transactional customers

High-usage, high-loyalty customers were also the most economically valuable segment of the transactional business. Aggressively converting them to subscription would create a painful transition period — cannibalizing the highest-margin customers before subscription scale could compensate. The decile matrix made this trade-off quantifiable for the first time.

The Analytical Core

10×10 Customer Decile Matrix

The matrix maps the installed customer base across two dimensions. Its development was triggered in part by the decision to eliminate the free 15-page subscription plan — a move that forced an honest accounting of which customer segments the paid plans actually served and at what revenue impact. monthly ink usage (vertical axis — how much ink a customer consumes per month, from near-zero at the bottom to heavy users at the top) and OEM loyalty (horizontal axis — what percentage of their cartridges are genuine HP vs. third-party or refilled, from lower loyalty on the left to near-100% on the right). Each cell is a customer segment. In the default Revenue Gap view, green cells are segments where HP collects more revenue from subscription than from trade; red cells are segments where conversion to subscription would cost HP revenue. Darker red = larger revenue loss.

Revenue Comparison

Annual revenue per printer unit: Transactional vs. Subscription

The revenue comparison tells a two-part story. For low-to-moderate usage customers (D1–D7), subscription plan prices exceed HP's 65% channel share of trade revenue — meaning subscription is actually better for HP revenue in that segment. For high-usage customers (D8–D10), the transactional model generates significantly more revenue, because high-usage customers buying large quantities of OEM cartridges at full retail price generate more HP revenue than any subscription plan priced to attract them. This created the core tension: the customers the subscription team most wanted to convert were the ones whose conversion was most costly to HP's revenue base.

Zone 1 — D1–D7
HP Wins — Sub Revenue Exceeds Trade
With the $0.99 entry plan (15pp), HP collects more from subscription than from the equivalent trade channel share for D1–D6. The 50pp plan at $2.99 covers D7 near break-even. These low-to-moderate usage customers are better for HP in subscription — not a primary targeting priority, but not harmful to convert.
Zone 2 — D8 (85pp)
2019 Gap — Resolved by Price Increase
At 2019 pricing, the 100pp plan at $4.99 falls $0.86/month short of HP's trade revenue equivalent. The post-resolution increase to $6.49 closes this gap and puts HP ahead by $0.64/month. Strong subscription value prop for the customer; viable for HP post-resolution.
Zone 3 — D9 (175pp)
2019 Gap — Resolved by Price Increase
At 2019 pricing, the 100pp plan with overages falls $2.16/month short of HP's trade revenue equivalent. The post-resolution 100pp plan at $6.49 with higher overage rates closes the gap. These are prime cannibalization risk customers — high usage, high value in trade — making the price increase critical before targeting them.
Zone 4 — D10 (280pp)
Structural Mismatch — Outside Scope of This Analysis
Very heavy users on entry-class hardware with a $175/yr revenue gap that plan price increases alone cannot close. Poor OEM loyalty (~89%) reflects cost pressure already pushing these customers toward aftermarket ink — a further signal that the printer class is wrong for the use case. A potential next step identified but not implemented: restricting the 300pp plan to Officejet printers (a platform with a lower cost-per-page configuration better suited to this usage level), which would signal to D10 customers that an upgrade is the appropriate path rather than a higher-tier subscription plan.
